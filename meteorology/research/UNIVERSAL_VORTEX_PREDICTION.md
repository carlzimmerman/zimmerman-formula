## 2. Test Systems

### 2.1 Jupiter's Great Red Spot

**What it is**: Anticyclonic storm in Jupiter's atmosphere, persisting for >400 years.

**Why it's relevant**:
- Long-lived → likely near equilibrium
- Well-observed by spacecraft (Voyager, Juno)
- Different atmosphere (H₂/He vs N₂/O₂)

**Observational data**:
- Size: ~16,000 km × 12,000 km (elongated)
- Maximum wind speed: ~120 m/s at edge
- Central region: relatively calm

**Prediction**: If 1/Z is universal:
```
r_calm_center / r_max_wind ≈ 0.173
```

For GRS (taking average radius ~14,000 km):
```
r_core ≈ 0.173 × 14,000 km ≈ 2,400 km
```

**Test**: Measure the radial wind profile from Juno data. Does the calm core extend to ~17% of the maximum wind radius?

**Complication**: GRS is elongated (not circular), so interpretation is less clean.

---

### 2.2 Saturn's Polar Vortex

**What it is**: Persistent cyclonic vortex at Saturn's north pole, with hexagonal jet stream.

**Why it's relevant**:
- Extremely long-lived (decades+)
- Nearly axisymmetric at the core
- Well-observed by Cassini

**Observational data**:
- Eye diameter: ~2,000 km
- Hurricane-like structure with clear eye
- Cloud-top winds: ~150 m/s

**Prediction**:
```
r_eye / r_max_wind ≈ 0.173
```

**Test**: Cassini wind profiles should show maximum winds at r ≈ 5.8 × r_eye.

**Status**: This is testable with published Cassini data.

---

### 2.3 Earth's Polar Vortex

**What it is**: Large-scale cyclonic circulation over polar regions in the stratosphere.

**Why it's relevant**:
- Quasi-permanent feature
- Important for weather/climate
- Well-observed by radiosondes and satellites

**Characteristics**:
- Diameter: ~1000 km (varies)
- Not as well-defined as tropical cyclones
- Subject to disruption (sudden stratospheric warmings)

**Prediction**: When undisturbed, the polar vortex core should be ~17% of the maximum wind radius.

**Complication**: The polar vortex is often disrupted and rarely in true equilibrium.

**Test**: Analyze ERA5 data for periods of stable polar vortex; measure core/max ratio.

---

### 2.4 Tornadoes

**What they are**: Small-scale, intense rotating columns of air.

**Why relevant**:
- Visible structure (funnel cloud ≈ core)
- High intensity → strong organization
- Short-lived but can reach quasi-steady state

**Characteristics**:
- Funnel diameter: 10-1000 m
- Max winds: 100-300 mph at edge
- Visible funnel ≈ region of low pressure/condensation

**Prediction**:
```
r_funnel / r_max_wind ≈ 0.173
```

If funnel is 50 m diameter, max winds at r ≈ 145 m.

**Test**: Doppler radar data from tornado intercepts. Compare funnel radius to radius of maximum winds.

**Complication**: Tornadoes are short-lived, may not reach equilibrium.

---

### 2.5 Dust Devils

**What they are**: Small rotating columns of air made visible by dust.

**Why relevant**:
- Observable on Earth and Mars
- Simpler dynamics (no latent heat release)
- Can study many samples

**Characteristics**:
- Diameter: 1-100 m
- Heights: 10-1000 m
- Duration: minutes

**Prediction**: Visible dust column should be ~17% of wind maximum radius.

**Test**: Video analysis of dust devils with anemometer measurements.

---

### 2.6 Laboratory Vortices

**What they are**: Controlled rotating flow experiments.

**Why relevant**:
- Controlled conditions
- Measurable with high precision
- Can vary parameters systematically

**Examples**:
- Taylor-Couette flow (between rotating cylinders)
- Bathtub vortex (draining fluid)
- Rotating tank experiments (simulating planetary atmospheres)

**Prediction**: In free-draining vortex (bathtub), the core radius should be ~17% of the maximum tangential velocity radius.

**Test**: PIV (Particle Image Velocimetry) measurements of rotating drain flow.

---

### 2.7 Accretion Disk Inner Edge

**What they are**: Disks of matter spiraling into compact objects (black holes, neutron stars).

**Why relevant**:
- Fundamental astrophysical system
- Well-studied theoretically and observationally
- Strong rotation

**Prediction**: If 1/Z is universal, the inner edge of the disk might relate to the radius of maximum orbital velocity by:
```
r_inner / r_vmax ≈ 0.173
```

**Complication**: Accretion disks have additional physics (magnetohydrodynamics, radiation).

**Test**: Compare observed inner disk radii to theoretical models.

---

### 2.8 Tokamak Plasma Rotation

**What it is**: Magnetically confined plasma in fusion reactors often rotates.

**Why relevant**:
- Highly controlled environment
- Precision diagnostics
- May show universal vortex properties

**Prediction**: If plasma rotation develops a vortex structure, core/max ratio → 0.173.

**Test**: Rotation profile measurements in tokamaks like ITER.

## 4. Why Systems Might Deviate

Even if 1/Z is fundamental, deviations could occur due to:

### 4.1 Non-Equilibrium
- System hasn't had time to relax to equilibrium
- External forcing maintains different state
- Example: Tornadoes (short-lived)

### 4.2 Non-Axisymmetry
- Elongated vortices (Jupiter GRS)
- Asymmetric forcing
- Example: GRS has 2:1 aspect ratio

### 4.3 Additional Physics
- Magnetic fields (MHD effects)
- Phase changes (latent heat)
- Compressibility (high Mach number)
- Example: Accretion disks (magnetic)

### 4.4 Boundary Effects
- Constrained geometry
- Surface effects
- Example: Lab vortices in finite containers
