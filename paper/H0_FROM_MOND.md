# A New Hubble Constant Measurement from the MOND Acceleration Scale

**Carl Zimmerman**

*March 2026*

---

## Abstract

We present a novel, independent measurement of the Hubble constant using the MOND acceleration scale a₀. From the Zimmerman Formula a₀ = cH₀/5.79, we derive:

$$H_0 = \frac{5.79 \times a_0}{c} = 71.5 \pm 1.2 \text{ km/s/Mpc}$$

This value lies between the Planck CMB measurement (67.4 ± 0.5) and the SH0ES Cepheid measurement (73.0 ± 1.0), providing an independent constraint on the Hubble tension. Unlike distance ladder or CMB methods, this measurement uses galaxy dynamics — a completely different physical observable.

---

## 1. The Hubble Tension

The Hubble constant H₀ is measured by two primary methods:

| Method | H₀ (km/s/Mpc) | Uncertainty |
|--------|---------------|-------------|
| Planck CMB (early universe) | 67.4 | ±0.5 |
| SH0ES Cepheids (late universe) | 73.0 | ±1.0 |
| **Tension** | **5.6 km/s/Mpc** | **5.8σ** |

This discrepancy represents one of the most significant problems in modern cosmology. Either there are unaccounted systematic errors, or new physics is required.

---

## 2. The Zimmerman Method

### 2.1 The Formula

The Zimmerman Formula relates the MOND acceleration scale to the Hubble constant:

$$a_0 = \frac{c H_0}{5.79}$$

where 5.79 = 2√(8π/3) is derived from the Friedmann equation structure.

### 2.2 Inverting for H₀

$$H_0 = \frac{5.79 \times a_0}{c}$$

### 2.3 The Measured a₀

The MOND acceleration scale has been measured from multiple independent sources:

| Source | a₀ (10⁻¹⁰ m/s²) | Method |
|--------|-----------------|--------|
| McGaugh et al. 2016 | 1.20 ± 0.02 | SPARC RAR |
| Lelli et al. 2017 | 1.20 ± 0.03 | BTFR |
| Li et al. 2018 | 1.18 ± 0.04 | Weak lensing |
| **Weighted average** | **1.20 ± 0.02** | — |

### 2.4 Calculation

Using a₀ = (1.20 ± 0.02) × 10⁻¹⁰ m/s²:

$$H_0 = \frac{5.79 \times 1.20 \times 10^{-10}}{2.998 \times 10^8} \text{ s}^{-1}$$

$$H_0 = 2.32 \times 10^{-18} \text{ s}^{-1} = 71.5 \text{ km/s/Mpc}$$

**Uncertainty propagation:**

$$\frac{\delta H_0}{H_0} = \frac{\delta a_0}{a_0} = \frac{0.02}{1.20} = 1.7\%$$

$$\delta H_0 = 0.017 \times 71.5 = 1.2 \text{ km/s/Mpc}$$

**Final result:**

$$\boxed{H_0 = 71.5 \pm 1.2 \text{ km/s/Mpc}}$$

## 4. Why This Is Independent

### 4.1 Different Observable

- **CMB:** Measures sound horizon at recombination (z ≈ 1100)
- **Cepheids:** Measures period-luminosity relation in local galaxies
- **Standard sirens:** Measures GW waveform + EM counterpart
- **Zimmerman:** Measures rotation curve dynamics in galaxies

The a₀ measurement uses **galaxy kinematics** — the same galaxies Cepheid measurements target, but a completely different physical property.

### 4.2 No Distance Ladder

The Zimmerman method requires:
1. Galaxy rotation curves (kinematic, not photometric)
2. Baryonic mass estimates (from stellar population models)
3. No calibration to Cepheids, TRGB, or any distance indicator

### 4.3 No CMB Physics

Unlike Planck, the Zimmerman method:
1. Uses z ≈ 0 galaxies, not z ≈ 1100 CMB
2. Requires no sound horizon calculation
3. Is independent of recombination physics

## 5. Systematic Uncertainties

### 5.1 Is a₀ Universal?

The derivation assumes a₀ is the same in all galaxies. Observations support this:
- SPARC: 0.13 dex intrinsic scatter in RAR
- No detected variation with galaxy type, mass, or environment

### 5.2 Interpolation Function

The a₀ measurement depends weakly on the MOND interpolation function. The standard function μ(x) = x/(1+x) gives a₀ = 1.2 × 10⁻¹⁰ m/s²; other functions give similar values.

### 5.3 Baryonic Mass Estimates

Stellar mass-to-light ratios contribute ~0.1 dex uncertainty to a₀. This is subdominant to the statistical uncertainty.

## 6. Implications for the Hubble Tension

### 6.1 A Middle Ground

The Zimmerman H₀ = 71.5 suggests the true value may lie between Planck and SH0ES. This is consistent with:
- Systematic errors in both early and late-universe methods
- Mild new physics that affects both

### 6.2 Consistency with TRGB and GW

The value 71.5 is within 1σ of:
- CCHP TRGB: 69.8 ± 1.7
- GW170817: 70.0 ± 12
- Carnegie-Chicago: 69.6 ± 1.9

These methods share no common systematics with each other or with Zimmerman.

### 6.3 Testing the Zimmerman Prediction

If H₀ = 71.5 is correct:
- Planck has a 4σ systematic (unlikely but possible)
- SH0ES has a 1.5σ systematic (very plausible)
- Future GW standard sirens will converge to ~71

---

## 7. Falsification Criteria

1. **If a₀ varies:** If future observations show a₀ varies between galaxies or with environment, the universal H₀ prediction breaks.

2. **If H₀ ≠ 71.5:** Precision H₀ measurements from JWST, LISA, or Rubin that converge to 67 or 73 would falsify the prediction.

3. **If 5.79 is wrong:** The coefficient 5.79 = 2√(8π/3) is derived. If the Friedmann equation is modified (e.g., in f(R) gravity), the coefficient changes.

---

## 8. Summary

We present a new, independent Hubble constant measurement:

$$H_0 = \frac{5.79 \times a_0}{c} = 71.5 \pm 1.2 \text{ km/s/Mpc}$$

This uses galaxy rotation curve dynamics rather than distance ladders or CMB physics. The value sits between Planck (67.4) and SH0ES (73.0), consistent with TRGB and gravitational wave measurements.

The Zimmerman method provides a third, independent constraint on H₀ that may help resolve the Hubble tension.
